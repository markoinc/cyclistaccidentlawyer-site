## 1. BUYER PERSONA SCENARIOS

### PERSONA A: Solo PI Attorney, 3 Years Experience, $200K/year, Currently on Wix, Never Worked with an Agency

**Would they buy?** MAYBE — but price sensitivity is HIGH.

**At what price?** Standard tier ($12,500) is the maximum. They'll flinch. At $200K/year revenue, their total marketing budget should be $20K-$40K (10-20% of revenue). $12,500 for a website is 31-63% of their ENTIRE marketing budget. They may need the 50/50 split to swallow it.

**What makes them say NO:**
- "I can't afford $12,500 right now" — they're still building their practice, every dollar is visible
- "I don't know if I need all that — my Wix site is 'fine'" — they don't know what they're missing because they've never had better
- "I don't have a vendor to 'fire' — so the liberation narrative doesn't apply to me" — the core positioning assumes they're escaping a bad vendor. This persona has no vendor to escape.
- No burn scars means no fear-driven urgency

**What makes them say YES faster:**
- A free audit showing their Wix site ranks nowhere and loads slowly
- The "one case pays for the website" math ($16,500 > $12,500)
- A payment plan — 3 payments of $4,500 makes it digestible
- Seeing a before/after mockup of their site
- Fear that a competitor in their market will lock them out via exclusivity

**Risk Level:** 3/5 — Moderate. They're a viable buyer but need more nurturing. The offer's "vendor liberation" framing partially misses them.

**Current offer response:** Adequate but not optimized. The offer is heavily framed around migration/liberation. A Wix DIY attorney doesn't need "liberation" — they need "transformation." The offer should have a secondary narrative for greenfield clients.

**Gap identified:** No explicit messaging for attorneys who DON'T have a vendor to fire. The "new build" use case needs its own 2-3 talking points.

### PERSONA B: Solo PI Attorney, 15 Years Experience, $600K/year, Currently on Scorpion at $5K/mo, Been Burned Twice

**Would they buy?** HIGH PROBABILITY — this is the DREAM prospect.

**At what price?** Professional tier ($18,500) without much pushback. They're spending $60K/year on Scorpion. $18,500 one-time is a no-brainer financially. They might even go Enterprise if you position it right.

**What makes them say NO:**
- "I've been burned twice. How do I know you're not just another slick pitch?" — Trust is the ONLY barrier
- "You're a young guy I've never heard of" — credibility gap vs. established agencies
- "Can you show me PI firms you've actually done this for?" — No case studies = dead on arrival
- "My Scorpion contract has 8 months left and a $10K ETF" — Timing/logistics

**What makes them say YES faster:**
- 2-3 PI-specific case studies with before/after metrics
- A reference call with another PI attorney client
- The vendor liberation service (handling the Scorpion breakup is HUGE — they dread it)
- The savings math: $60K/year to Scorpion → $18,500 once + $5K/mo retainer = $78,500 year 1, then $60K/year ongoing. They break even in Year 1 and save $60K/year every year after that vs. the website-hostage model.
- Geographic exclusivity — "your competitors can't hire me"
- The 30-day satisfaction guarantee eliminates their burn fear
- Month-to-month retainer (anti-Scorpion positioning)

**Risk Level:** 1/5 — Low risk. This buyer persona is perfectly aligned with the offer.

**Current offer response:** EXCELLENT. The offer was basically designed for this person. Every element — migration, vendor firing, ownership, guarantees, exclusivity, month-to-month — addresses their specific pain.

### PERSONA C: Small Firm (3 Attorneys), $2M Revenue, on FindLaw, Managing Partner Decides, Burned Once

**Would they buy?** YES — strong probability.

**At what price?** Professional ($18,500) or Enterprise ($27,500) depending on site complexity. At $2M revenue, they should be spending $200K-$400K/year on marketing. This is a rounding error.

**What makes them say NO:**
- "I need to discuss with my partners" — multi-stakeholder decision slows things down
- "Our FindLaw contract isn't up for 6 months" — Timing objection
- "Who are you? We've never heard of Kurios" — Brand awareness gap
- "We've been looking at Rankings.io" — if they're comparing to a $200K/year agency, you look small

**What makes them say YES faster:**
- A proposal document they can share with partners (the offer document is great for this)
- The savings math against FindLaw's ongoing costs
- Exclusivity framing — "your competitor down the road is talking to us too"
- Having the managing partner realize their FindLaw content isn't even owned by them
- A clear migration timeline that works around their FindLaw contract end date

**Risk Level:** 2/5 — Low-moderate. Main risk is slower decision cycle (partner buy-in) and potential comparison shopping.

**Current offer response:** Strong. The FindLaw-specific playbook in the offer document is good. Consider adding a "partner presentation deck" as a sales tool.

### PERSONA D: Mid-Size Firm (8 Attorneys), $5M Revenue, In-House Marketing Person, Custom WordPress, Considering Rankings.io

**Would they buy?** UNLIKELY at current positioning.

**At what price?** Price isn't the issue — credibility and scope are. They're evaluating $100K-$200K/year agencies. Your offer looks too small for them.

**What makes them say NO:**
- "We already have an in-house marketing person — they think you're a threat to their job"
- "We're talking to Rankings.io, who has a huge brand and conference and podcast"
- "We already own our WordPress site — what are you actually adding?"
- "You're building websites. We need a comprehensive digital marketing strategy, not just a site."
- "Our marketing coordinator looked you up and you don't have enough of a track record"

**What makes them say YES faster:**
- Position as a partner to their marketing person, not a replacement
- The Enterprise tier + Domination retainer ($27,500 + $7,500/mo = $117,500/year) is in Rankings.io territory
- Geographic exclusivity that Rankings.io doesn't consistently guarantee
- If their current WordPress site is slow, ugly, or not converting — a rebuild makes sense
- If they hate Rankings.io's pricing opacity

**Risk Level:** 4/5 — High. This persona is at the edge of the offer's natural market. The in-house marketer is a gatekeeper who may actively sabotage the sale.

**Current offer response:** Weak for this persona. The offer positions against Scorpion/FindLaw (vendor hostages), not against "upgrading from an already-owned WordPress site." There's no clear narrative for firms that already own their site and just want it rebuilt better/faster.

**Gap identified:** Need a "rebuild" narrative for firms that already own their infrastructure but need modernization, not liberation.

### PERSONA E: New Firm, Zero Online Presence, Desperate for Cases, $50K Marketing Budget Year 1

**Would they buy?** YES — but budget is TIGHT.

**At what price?** Standard tier ($12,500) is the only realistic option. At a $50K total Year 1 marketing budget, spending $12,500 on a website leaves only $37,500 for everything else (SEO, ads, directories, networking). If they add the $5K/mo retainer, that's $50K for 10 months of SEO. Total: $62,500 — they've already blown their budget.

**What makes them say NO:**
- "I need that $12,500 for Google Ads to get cases NOW" — new firms need immediate revenue, not a long-term SEO play
- "Can you do it for $7,500?" — budget pressure
- "I don't have an existing site to 'migrate' — do I still get the same value?"
- "I need cases in 30 days, not leads in 90 days"

**What makes them say YES faster:**
- If you explicitly frame the website as a case-generation machine, not just a digital presence
- If you can show them how SEO + Google Ads + a great website work together
- If you offer a "startup rate" or reduced scope for new firms (e.g., 8-page site at $9,500)
- If you bundle Google Ads setup with the website build
- The 90-day lead guarantee is actually perfect for them — they NEED that assurance

**Risk Level:** 3/5 — Moderate. They're motivated but cash-constrained. High risk of being unable to afford the retainer.

**Current offer response:** Partially adequate. The Standard tier works for them, but the migration/liberation framing doesn't apply. There's no "new firm" pricing or narrative path.

**Gap identified:** Should there be a sub-$12,500 entry point for firms with ZERO existing web presence? A "Launch" tier at $8,500-$9,500 for 5-10 pages with no migration required could capture this segment without devaluing the core offer. The current document says "$12,500 is the floor. No project goes below this." This is leaving money on the table from startup firms.

**Current offer response:** Good. The sales framework's emphasis on leading with exclusivity and stacking guarantees is exactly right for competitive situations. The 30-day delivery advantage is a strong closer.

### PERSONA H: Skeptic Attorney, Thinks Marketing is a Scam, Only Does Referrals, Partner is Pushing Them Online

**Would they buy?** UNLIKELY without significant persuasion.

**At what price?** Any price feels like a waste to them. They don't believe in online marketing.

**What makes them say NO:**
- "I've gotten every case from referrals for 20 years. Why would I change now?"

**Gap identified:** Add a "referral validation" talking point to the sales framework: "96% of potential clients Google a lawyer before calling — even when they were referred. If they Google your name and find a terrible website, you're losing referrals you already earned."

## 2. PRICING SCENARIOS

### Scenario 2.1: Competitor Launches $5,000 "AI Website for Lawyers" Tomorrow

**What happens:** A well-funded startup (or an existing player like Durable, 10Web, or a new entrant) launches a $5,000 "AI-powered website for lawyers" with decent design, basic SEO, and 48-hour delivery.

**Risk Level:** 3/5

**Current offer's response:** The offer explicitly says "never say AI-built" and positions on proprietary technology + human strategy. The $12,500 floor creates a 2.5x price gap vs. the $5K product.

**Is the response adequate?** Mostly yes, but with a vulnerability. If the $5K product is "good enough" visually and functionally, price-sensitive buyers (Personas A, E, H) will drift toward it. The offer's response must emphasize:
- Custom strategy vs. template generation
- PI-specific expertise vs. generic legal
- Full migration service (the $5K product won't migrate your Scorpion site)
- Geographic exclusivity (the $5K product serves everyone)
- Performance guarantees (the $5K product won't guarantee leads)

**What should change:** Nothing structurally. But the sales framework needs a "vs. cheap AI sites" objection handler added. Something like: *"Yes, you can get an AI-generated website for $5K. You can also use a $500 AI tool to draft a legal brief. Would you file it without review? Our system uses technology for efficiency, but every strategic decision — your market positioning, your keyword targets, your competitive differentiation — is driven by humans who specialize in PI."*

---

### Scenario 2.2: Rankings.io Starts Offering a $15K Quick-Build Option

**What happens:** Rankings.io leverages their brand to offer a streamlined website product — $15,000, 60-day delivery, basic SEO setup, but with their logo and credibility behind it.

**Risk Level:** 4/5

**Current offer's response:** This would be a direct competitive threat at nearly identical pricing. Rankings.io's brand advantage in PI is massive.

**Is the response adequate?** NO. If Rankings.io enters this price point, the current offer loses its primary differentiators:
- Rankings.io already has PI credibility
- Rankings.io already has case studies
- Rankings.io can match the price
- Rankings.io has Chris Dreyer's personal brand

**What should change:**
1. Double down on geographic exclusivity (Rankings.io is "semi-exclusive" at best)
2. Double down on speed (30 days vs. 60 days)
3. Double down on guarantees (Rankings.io offers ZERO guarantees)
4. Double down on the anti-hostage, full-ownership messaging
5. Consider: Is this a scenario where you LOWER the price to $9,500-$10,000 to create clear daylight? Or do you RAISE it to $18,500 and compete on value?

**Recommended response:** RAISE the Standard to $15,000 and lead aggressively with the guarantee stack + exclusivity + 30-day delivery. Don't compete with Rankings.io on price — compete on structure. They'll never offer a 30-day satisfaction guarantee. They'll never guarantee 10 leads in 90 days. They'll never fire your vendor for you.

### Scenario 2.3: First Client Asks for a Discount — What's the Floor?

**What happens:** A qualified prospect says "I love this but can you do $10,000?"

**Risk Level:** 2/5 (if handled correctly)

**Current offer's response:** The document says "$12,500 is the floor. No project goes below this." It also says "never discount the core price — add value instead."

**Is the response adequate?** YES, philosophically. But the offer needs a clearer "value add" menu for discount requests:
- Add an extra month of post-launch support (costs you nothing)
- Include 2 additional blog posts (costs you $200-400 in production)
- Throw in a 60-minute strategy call at the 90-day mark
- Offer the 50/50 payment split if they haven't seen it yet

**The true floor should be $10,000** for exceptional circumstances (loss leader into a high-value market, referral partner, first PI client for case study purposes). But this should NEVER be advertised or easy to get. It should feel like pulling teeth.

**What should change:** Add a "negotiation playbook" to the sales framework with 3-4 value-add alternatives to discounting.

### Scenario 2.4: Should There Be a "Startup" Price for New Firms (0 Existing Pages)?

**What happens:** A brand new firm with no website approaches. They don't need migration, don't need vendor cancellation, don't need 301 redirects. They need 8-10 pages from scratch.

**Risk Level:** 2/5

**Current offer's response:** The offer says $12,500 is the floor regardless. But the value proposition is partially hollow for this buyer — 30-40% of the deliverables (migration, vendor firing, redirect mapping, SEO equity preservation) don't apply.

**Is the response adequate?** NO. The perceived value mismatch is real. A new firm getting the same price as a Scorpion escapee but receiving less work will feel overcharged when they figure it out (and lawyers always figure it out).

**What should change:** Two options:
- **Option A (Recommended):** Keep $12,500 floor but explicitly add NEW value for new firms: expanded content (extra blog posts, case results pages with placeholder framework, more practice area pages), a Google Ads audit/setup consultation, a more comprehensive content calendar (6 months instead of 90 days), or a founding client discount with testimonial commitment.
- **Option B:** Create a "Launch" tier at $9,500 for new firms with 0-10 pages, no migration. Simple, clean, honest pricing that reflects the actual scope. Risk: cannibalization of the Standard tier.

### Scenario 2.5: Is $12,500 Too Low for Standard? Would $15,000 Convert Equally Well?

**What happens:** You test raising Standard from $12,500 to $15,000.

**Risk Level:** 1/5

**Current offer's response:** N/A — this is a pricing optimization question.

**Analysis:**
- The price perception research explicitly recommends $15,000-$20,000 as the website price range
- Rankings.io categorizes $10K-$15K as "low-tier" — being at the top of "low-tier" is worse than being at the bottom of "mid-tier"
- The research says PI lawyers associate higher prices with quality: *"$15K puts you solidly in mid-tier, not the budget/mid-tier boundary"*
- At $15,000, the "one case pays for it" math still works ($16,500 > $15,000)
- The $2,500 increase goes straight to margin (COGS don't change)
- Conversion rate likely stays flat or IMPROVES — price signals quality to sophisticated buyers

**Verdict: YES, $12,500 is too low for the Standard tier.** $15,000 is better positioning. The $12,500 price creates a perception risk that the $2,500 in additional revenue per deal more than compensates for. At 2 clients/month, that's an extra $60K/year.

**What should change:** Raise Standard to $15,000. Raise Professional to $20,000. Keep Enterprise at $27,500 (already well-positioned). This creates cleaner tier spacing: $15K / $20K / $27.5K.

### Scenario 2.6: Is $27,500 Too High for Enterprise? Would $22,500 Get More Volume?

**What happens:** Enterprise prospects consistently ask for Professional instead, citing the $9,000 gap ($18,500 → $27,500) as too large.

**Risk Level:** 2/5

**Analysis:**
- The gap between Professional and Enterprise is $9,000 (49% increase). The gap between Standard and Professional is $6,000 (48% increase). The relative jumps are actually proportional.
- The Enterprise buyer ($5M+ firm) has the budget. $27,500 is still dramatically cheaper than their current spend.
- However, the jump from 39 pages to 40+ pages triggering a $9,000 increase feels arbitrary. A firm with 42 pages pays $9,000 more than a firm with 38 pages.

**Verdict:** $27,500 is fine for true enterprise (60+ pages). But the tier boundary is too sharp. Consider:
- Adding a 4th tier or smoothing the transition: 40-59 pages at $24,000, 60+ pages at $30,000
- Or: Keep the 3 tiers but make page count a softer guideline, not a hard cutoff. "Based on complexity and scope, we'll recommend the right tier."

**What should change:** Make page count a "starting guideline" rather than a hard trigger. Allow flexibility in the 35-45 page zone.

### Scenario 2.7: $0 Upfront, $X/Month for 12 Months (Financing Model)

**What happens:** You offer a $0-down, $1,250/month × 12 = $15,000 total payment plan for the Standard tier.

**Risk Level:** 3/5

**Analysis:**
- **Pro:** Dramatically lowers the barrier to entry. Many PI firms are cash-flow conscious.
- **Pro:** Monthly payments feel like "replacing" their Scorpion/FindLaw monthly bill — same line item, different vendor
- **Pro:** Could increase conversion by 30-50% among budget-sensitive buyers (Personas A, E)
- **Con:** Collections risk. Lawyers are actually GOOD at not paying if they decide they don't owe you.
- **Con:** Cash flow hit — you're fronting 100% of COGS before receiving full payment
- **Con:** If the client is unhappy at month 3, you have 9 months of collections risk
- **Con:** Complicates the guarantee structure — how does a "30-day satisfaction refund" work when they've only paid $1,250?

**Verdict:** Don't offer this as a standard option. The 50/50 split and 3-payment plan are sufficient. BUT — for strategic deals in high-value markets, consider a 3-month payment plan with no premium: $5,000/mo × 3 = $15,000. Shorter term = lower risk.

**What should change:** Keep current payment structures. Add a 3-month equal installment option ($5,000 × 3 for Standard) as an alternative to the 3-payment premium plan, for strategic situations only.

## 3. COMPETITIVE RESPONSE SCENARIOS

### Scenario 3.1: Scorpion Lowers Prices and Goes Month-to-Month

**What happens:** Scorpion, under competitive pressure, introduces a $2,000/mo month-to-month option. Still proprietary CMS, but no contract lock-in.

**Risk Level:** 3/5

**Current offer's response:** If Scorpion goes month-to-month, one of your key differentiators weakens. But the core narrative survives because:
- They still use proprietary CMS (you still can't take your site)
- They still work with your competitors
- $2,000/mo × 12 = $24,000/year and you own NOTHING. $15,000 once and you own everything.
- Their sites still load slowly on CMS-8

**Is the response adequate?** Yes, but the messaging needs to shift emphasis from "no contracts" to "you own NOTHING even at the lower price." The financial math becomes: *"$2K/mo month-to-month sounds flexible, but after 12 months you've spent $24K and you still own nothing. Cancel and you start from zero. With us, you spend $15K once, you own it forever."*

---

### Scenario 3.2: Client's Current Vendor Offers to Match/Beat Your Price to Retain Them

**What happens:** After your sales call, the prospect tells their current vendor (Scorpion/FindLaw/whoever) they're leaving. The vendor panics and offers: "We'll cut your monthly to $1,500/mo and redesign your site for free."

**Risk Level:** 4/5 — This is very common and very effective at killing deals.

**Current offer's response:** The offer doesn't have a specific counter for this scenario.

**Is the response adequate?** NO. This is a critical gap.

**What should change:** Add a "vendor retention counter" to the objection handlers:

*"Of course they're offering you a discount now — they're about to lose a paying customer. Ask yourself: if they could have given you this deal before, why didn't they? And more importantly: even at $1,500/month, you still don't own your website. You're still on their proprietary platform. They're still working with your competitors. And in 12 months at $1,500/mo, you'll have spent $18,000 — more than our one-time fee — and you STILL won't own anything. Take their counter-offer, and you're back in this exact situation in 2 years, except you'll have spent $36,000 more. Come with us, spend $15,000 once, and never have this conversation again."*

---

### Scenario 3.3: FindLaw Starts Offering Site Ownership

**What happens:** FindLaw modernizes their platform — builds on WordPress, lets clients own the site, goes month-to-month.

**Risk Level:** 2/5 (unlikely in near-term; Thomson Reuters moves slowly)

**Current offer's response:** If FindLaw offered ownership, your differentiation shifts to:
- Speed (30 days vs. FindLaw's notoriously slow timelines)
- Quality (custom vs. template)
- PI specialization (FindLaw is a generalist)
- Geographic exclusivity (FindLaw serves everyone)
- Performance guarantees (FindLaw guarantees nothing)

**Is the response adequate?** Yes. Even if FindLaw copied the ownership model, they can't copy exclusivity, speed, guarantees, and PI expertise simultaneously. Moat holds.

### Scenario 3.4: Cheaper Competitor Copies Your Exact Offer at 50% of Your Price

**What happens:** A solo developer or small agency sees your offer, copies the framework, positioning, even the guarantee language, and offers it at $6,250.

**Risk Level:** 3/5

**Current offer's response:** Your moat against copycat competition is:
1. **Execution quality** — Can they actually deliver custom PI websites in 30 days?
2. **Case studies** — Do they have PI-specific proof?
3. **Exclusivity at scale** — Can they maintain one-per-market as they grow?
4. **Guarantee fulfillment** — Can they afford to honor a 90-day performance guarantee?
5. **Your personal brand and relationship network**

## 7. LEGAL/COMPLIANCE SCENARIOS

### Scenario 7.1: Bar Association Restrictions on Marketing Claims

**What happens:** A lawyer's state bar prohibits "guarantees" in marketing. The attorney can't use guarantee language on their own marketing, and they question whether YOUR guarantee language could create issues.

**Risk Level:** 3/5

**Analysis:**
- The guarantees in this offer are about YOUR service delivery, not the attorney's marketing to clients

- ABA Model Rules 7.1 prohibit "false or misleading" marketing. A marketing agency's guarantee to the law firm is not regulated by the bar — it's a B2B service contract. But the PERCEPTION matters to risk-averse lawyers.

**What should change:**
- Add a footnote/disclaimer: "These guarantees are part of our service agreement with your firm and are not related to your firm's marketing claims to potential clients."

## 11. 5 SPECIFIC RECOMMENDED CHANGES

### CHANGE 1: Fix the 90-Day Performance Guarantee (URGENT)
**Current:** "10 qualified leads in 90 days or we keep working for free until you hit that number."

**Why:** The current guarantee is an open-ended financial liability that makes a promise organic SEO cannot reliably keep in 90 days. ### CHANGE 2: Raise Standard Tier to $15,000
**Current:** $12,500 / $18,500 / $27,500
**Recommended:** $15,000 / $20,000 / $27,500

**Why:** $12,500 sits at the credibility boundary between "low-tier" and "mid-tier" per Rankings.io's own framework. $15,000 puts you solidly in mid-tier. ### CHANGE 3: Tie Geographic Exclusivity to Ongoing Engagement
**Current:** Exclusivity is active "as long as the client maintains an active relationship (website delivered or retainer active)."

**Why:** Exclusivity is your most valuable strategic asset. Giving it away indefinitely for a one-time fee is underpricing it. **Why:** 15-20% of prospects will be new firms, career switchers, or DIY-ers. The "liberation" narrative doesn't resonate with them. Having a parallel narrative captures this segment without changing the offer structure.
